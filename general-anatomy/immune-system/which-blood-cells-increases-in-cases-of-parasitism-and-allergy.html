Which blood cells increases in cases of parasitism and allergy?

(A) basophil

(B) ** eosinophil

(C) lymphocyte

(D) monocyte

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -Definition. Eosinophilia (e-o-sin-o-FILL-e-uh) is a higher than normal level of eosinophils. Eosinophils are a type of disease-fighting white blood cell. This condition most often indicates a parasitic infection, an allergic reaction or cancer.

Concept note-2: -During parasitic infections, the numbers of peripheral blood eosinophils are highly increased under the influence of Th2 cell-derived IL-5, IL-3 and GM-CSF, and eosinophils are recruited from the circulation into inflamed or damaged tissues by the eosinophil selective chemokine, eotaxin [2].

Concept note-3: -Eosinophilia occurs when a large number of eosinophils are recruited to a specific site in your body or when the bone marrow produces too many eosinophils. This can be caused by a variety of factors, including: Parasitic and fungal diseases. Allergic reactions.

Concept note-4: -For over 100 years, the eosinophil has been associated with allergic disease. At present, eosinophils appear to be associated pathologically with asthma, atopic dermatitis, allergic rhinitis, eosinophilic gastroenteritis, and certain eye diseases.

Concept note-5: -Basophils: These represent less than 1% of white blood cells in the body and are typically present in increased numbers after an allergic reaction. Eosinophils: These are responsible for responding to infections that parasites cause.
