Where the skull plates meet is called

(A) gaps

(B) ** sutures

(C) cartilage

(D) epiphysis

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -The skull of an infant or young child is made up of bony plates that allow for growth of the skull. The borders where these plates intersect are called sutures or suture lines.

Concept note-2: -Cranial sutures are fibrous bands of tissue that connect the bones of the skull. The sutures or anatomical lines where the bony plates of the skull join together can be easily felt in the newborn infant.

Concept note-3: -Joints made of strong, fibrous tissue (cranial sutures) hold the bones of your baby’s skull together. The sutures meet at the fontanels, the soft spots on your baby’s head. The sutures remain flexible during infancy, allowing the skull to expand as the brain grows. The largest fontanel is at the front (anterior).

Concept note-4: -The sutures of the skull, also referred to as the cranial sutures, are fibrous joints that connect the bones of the skull. They appear as intricate thin lines that mark the adherence between the bones and the growth and closure of the cranial fontanelles.

Concept note-5: -Most of the bones of the skull are held together by firm, immovable fibrous joints called sutures or synarthroses. These joints allow the developing skull to grow both pre-and postnatally.
